\section{Introduction}
This assignment studies how rainfall shocks affect economic outcomes using a geospatial panel for
India. The design follows three steps: (i) aggregate gridded ERA5 climate data to administrative
boundaries, (ii) construct a climate shock measure from long-run climatology, and (iii) estimate
panel regressions with fixed effects.

\section{Data}
\subsection{Climate data}
The climate variable is daily \texttt{total\_precipitation} from ERA5 reanalysis over India.
State-level administrative boundaries are taken from GADM (level-1). Raster and vector layers are
aligned in a common CRS before zonal extraction.

\subsection{Economic outcome}
The empirical script is outcome-agnostic and accepts any state-month economic panel with columns
\texttt{unit\_id}, \texttt{ym}, and \texttt{y\_outcome}. Examples include state-level food inflation,
state-level labor-market outcomes, or state-level agricultural market indicators.

\section{Geospatial processing and zonal statistics}
Let $p_{itd}$ be daily precipitation in state $i$ on day $d$ in month $t$.
The pipeline:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item loads ERA5 netCDF as a multi-layer raster,
  \item loads state polygons and transforms them to raster CRS,
  \item computes area-weighted zonal means for each day and state,
  \item converts ERA5 precipitation from meters to millimeters.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Climate shock construction}
Monthly mean precipitation:
\begin{equation}
P_{it} = \frac{1}{D_t}\sum_{d=1}^{D_t} p_{itd},
\end{equation}
where $D_t$ is days in month $t$.

Month-specific long-run climatology for each state:
\begin{equation}
\bar P_{i,m} = \mathbb{E}[P_{it}\mid \text{calendar month}=m], \qquad
\sigma_{i,m} = SD(P_{it}\mid \text{calendar month}=m).
\end{equation}

Two shock variables:
\begin{align}
\text{Anomaly}_{it} &= P_{it} - \bar P_{i,m}, \\
\text{zShock}_{it} &= \frac{P_{it} - \bar P_{i,m}}{\sigma_{i,m}}.
\end{align}

Robustness variable (extreme precipitation):
\begin{equation}
HR_{it}=\sum_{d=1}^{D_t}\mathbf{1}(p_{itd}>50\text{ mm}).
\end{equation}

\section{Empirical strategy}
\subsection{Baseline panel model}
\begin{equation}
Y_{it} = \beta \cdot \text{Shock}_{it} + \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \varepsilon_{it},
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_i$ are state fixed effects and $\gamma_t$ are month-time fixed effects. Standard errors are clustered at the state level.

\subsection{Dynamic response (local projection style)}
\begin{equation}
Y_{i,t+h} = \beta_h \cdot \text{zShock}_{it} + \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \varepsilon_{i,t+h},
\qquad h=0,\dots,6.
\end{equation}
This traces the response over six subsequent months.

\section{Identification discussion}
The identifying variation is within-state over time, net of aggregate month shocks. Key concerns:
\begin{itemize}
  \item omitted state-varying confounders (address via robustness and controls where feasible),
  \item serial correlation in panel errors (cluster by state),
  \item measurement noise in gridded weather and administrative overlays.
\end{itemize}

\section{Implementation and reproducibility}
Code is in \texttt{code/climate\_econ\_assignment.R}. The script writes clean intermediate datasets
and final outputs in \texttt{data/clean/} and \texttt{outputs/}. This structure ensures the workflow can
be rerun with alternative economic outcomes or a finer geography (district-level) with minimal changes.

\section{Conclusion}
This assignment provides a reproducible geospatial-econometric workflow linking climate variability to
macroeconomic outcomes. The design is suitable for extension to nonlinear effects, state-specific trends,
and policy-relevant heterogeneity analyses.
